# LAPS Sales Notes

## Healthcare + Medical Education Version

> **Core idea:**
> Attention → Lead → Appointment → Presentation → Offer → Follow-up → Reactivation → Revenue

---

# 1) Whole System Map

## LAPS =

* **L**eads
* **A**ppointments
* **P**resentations
* **S**ales

## What it really means

You are not “doing sales.”
You are building a **repeatable pipeline**.


# 2) Lead Generation

## Meaning

A lead is someone who:

1. notices you
2. knows you
3. signals interest

## 3-part lead system

### A) Short-form content = “Notice you”

Goal: get attention fast.

#### Clinics

Use pain-based hooks:

* missed calls
* slow replies
* patient leakage
* bad follow-up
* receptionist overload
* empty OPD slots

#### Medical students

Use pain-based hooks:

* forgetting
* poor revision
* weak recall
* random study methods
* burnout
* bad retention

### Short content rules

* one strong pain
* one strong outcome
* one clear CTA
* no generic educational garbage

### B) Long-form content = “Know you”

Goal: build trust.

Examples:

* workshop
* webinar
* demo
* case study
* podcast
* report
* detailed breakdown

#### Clinics

* clinic workflow demo
* WhatsApp automation demo
* patient booking system explanation

#### Students

* memory / revision system demo
* recall workflow
* study process breakdown

### C) Signal of interest = lead capture

A person becomes useful only when they do something:

* form fill
* waitlist signup
* webinar registration
* assessment
* booking request
* WhatsApp opt-in

## Lead capture architecture

```text
Content
  ↓
Landing page
  ↓
Form / assessment / waitlist
  ↓
CRM / sheet
```

# 3) Appointment Booking

## Meaning

Appointment booking = turning interest into **time on calendar**.

## Structure

1. reaffirm relevance
2. ask permission if needed
3. hook pitch
4. set the appointment

## Healthcare version

### Clinics

Example:

> You filled out the interest form about clinic workflow. I help clinics reduce missed bookings and automate follow-up. Let us book a short demo. Tomorrow morning or evening?

### Medical students

Example:

> You checked the study system. I help students improve retention and revision. Let us book a quick walkthrough.

## Appointment flow

```text
Context
  ↓
Relevance
  ↓
Short value statement
  ↓
Specific time request
```

## Qualification questions

Use these before wasting time:

* what is the current situation?
* what do they want?
* what are they using now?
* what is blocking them?
* what is the timeline?

### Clinic questions

* daily patient count
* specialty
* current booking system
* main bottleneck

### Student questions

* exam target
* current score
* revision method
* biggest struggle

# 4) Sales Presentation

## Meaning

This is the money stage.

The presentation is where the buyer decides:

* “this is for me”
* or “not now”

## Correct order

```text
Listen
  ↓
Diagnose
  ↓
Insight
  ↓
Method
  ↓
Solution
  ↓
Offer
```

## What weak sellers do

* jump directly to product
* explain features too early
* talk too much
* never diagnose properly

## What strong sellers do

* ask questions first
* understand the problem deeply
* reframe the problem
* show the system
* then show the solution

---

# 5) The 10 Presentation Components

## 1. Framing

Make the meeting look professional before you speak.

Examples:

* clean background
* good camera
* proper deck
* calm setup
* branded screen

Bad framing kills trust fast.

## 2. Rapport

Short human connection. Not 30 minutes of chatting.

Use:

* name
* common context
* polite tone
* small warmth

## 3. Permission / structure

Tell them the process.

Example:

> I will ask a few questions first, then I will show you what fits best.

## 4. Present situation

What is happening now?

## 5. Desired outcome / prize

What do they actually want?

## 6. Problems / obstacles

What blocks them?

## 7. Insight

Give a big-picture explanation that changes how they see the issue.

## 8. Method

Show the framework or process.

## 9. Solution

Now show the actual product / service / system.

## 10. Offer packaging

Give clear options.

---

# 6) The 3-Basket Model

Fill these before pitching.

## Basket 1: Present situation

* where are they now?
* what is broken?
* what have they tried?

## Basket 2: Prize

* what do they really want?
* what outcome matters?

## Basket 3: Problems

* what is stopping them?
* what criteria matter?
* what has failed before?

### Rule

If the baskets are empty, the pitch misses.

# 7) Insight → Method → Solution

This is the most important sales rule in the whole system.

## Insight

A big-picture idea that helps them understand their own problem better.

## Method

A repeatable framework that solves the problem.

## Solution

Your actual product / service / system.

### Correct order

```text
Insight
  ↓
Method
  ↓
Solution
```

### Wrong order

```text
Solution first
  ↓
Confusion
  ↓
Low trust
  ↓
Low conversion
```

## Healthcare examples

### Clinics

* Insight: the issue is system leakage, not just low demand.
* Method: capture → reply → follow-up → booking.
* Solution: AI receptionist / WhatsApp workflow / automation.

### Medical students

* Insight: the issue is not effort, it is poor recall structure.
* Method: study → compress → recall → test → repeat.
* Solution: revision system / AI study workflow / recall engine.

# 11) Making the Offer

## Meaning

After insight, method, and solution, you ask for the decision.

## Trap: conversational tennis

This is the ugly back-and-forth:

* objection
* answer
* objection
* answer
* repeat until both sides are tired

## Better approach: ball boy style

* catch objections
* do not fight immediately
* collect all concerns
* answer them together

### Offer flow

```text
Present solution
  ↓
Ask for decision
  ↓
Capture objections
  ↓
Group objections
  ↓
Answer together
  ↓
Close
```

## Example objection handling

Client says:

* price is high
* timing is bad
* needs approval

Your response:

* note all of it
* then answer all of it in one structured reply

### Rule

Stay in control. Do not spiral into endless Q&A.

# 13) Follow-Up

This is where a lot of money is won.

## Timing

The first 24–48 hours matter the most.

## Follow-up sequence

```text
Presentation
  ↓
Immediate note
  ↓
24h follow-up
  ↓
48h follow-up
  ↓
1 week follow-up
  ↓
2 week follow-up
  ↓
Longer follow-up if still relevant
```

## What follow-up should contain

* new insight
* case study
* testimonial
* relevant data
* reminder of the pain
* answer to a concern

## What not to do

* “just checking in”
* “any update?”
* random nudging with no value

## Rule

Follow-up must have a reason.

# 14) Completion vs Follow-Up

## If they are ready now

Complete the sale.

## If they are not ready now

Schedule the next checkpoint immediately.

### Bad

* “let me know later”

### Good

* “what time should we reconnect?”

# 15) Reactivation Campaigns

## Meaning

Bring silent or dead leads back.

These are people who:

* showed interest before
* disappeared
* never converted

## Reactivation message

Direct and simple:

> Have you given up on this?

Or:

> We have not heard from you for a while. Is this still important to you?

## If they are still interested

Send a small re-entry step:

* assessment
* calculator
* short form
* quick audit

## If they are not interested

Remove them from active follow-up.

### Reactivation flow

```text
Silent lead
  ↓
Reactivation message
  ↓
Soft signal of interest
  ↓
Assessment / calculator / form
  ↓
Back into pipeline
```

# 22) What to Reject as Bad Advice

You need explicit rejection of bad habits. Keep these out of your process.

## Bad habit 1: “Always be closing”

Wrong.
If you push for the close before diagnosis, you look desperate and weak.

### Better:

* diagnose first
* create clarity
* then close

## Bad habit 2: “Overcome every objection with more talking”

Wrong.
More talking is often just panic.

### Better:

* collect objections
* group them
* answer them once, clearly

## Bad habit 3: “Feature dumping”

Wrong.
Nobody cares about your feature list before they understand the problem.

### Better:

* insight first
* method second
* solution third

## Bad habit 4: “Play it cool”

Wrong.
Speed beats mystery.

### Better:

* follow up fast
* be responsive
* stay relevant

## Bad habit 5: “Random content”

Wrong.
Random content builds noise, not pipeline.

### Better:

* pain-based hooks
* clear CTA
* lead capture

## Bad habit 6: “Let the prospect decide the next step”

Wrong.
If you do not control the next action, the deal drifts.

### Better:

* book the next step now
* put it on calendar

# 23) Pricing and Negotiation

This is the part that needed more depth.

## 1. Competitor is cheaper

### Bad reaction

* panic
* drop price immediately
* defend for 10 minutes

### Better reaction

Ask:

* cheaper on what?
* what exactly are they comparing?
* what outcome do they expect?
* what risk are they ignoring?

### Positioning frame

Do not compete only on price. Compete on:

* speed
* reliability
* support
* outcome
* implementation quality
* reduced risk

### Simple response

> They may be cheaper on headline price. The real question is what happens after implementation, support, and follow-through.

---

## 2. Discounting strategy

### Rule

Do not discount randomly.

Discount only if:

* scope changes
* payment terms improve
* commitment increases
* package is simplified

### Better alternatives to discounting

* reduce scope
* remove support level
* shorten duration
* ask for upfront payment
* switch from custom to standard package

### Weak move

> I can lower price if needed.

### Strong move

> If budget is the issue, we can adjust scope or package structure.

---

## 3. Annual vs monthly anchoring

Always anchor intelligently.

### Annual offer advantages

* lower churn
* better commitment
* easier planning
* stronger ROI framing

### Monthly offer advantages

* lower entry friction
* easier start
* less fear

### Good pricing structure

Show annual first if it makes sense.

Example:

* monthly: higher
* annual: lower per month, better value

### Psychological effect

The annual plan makes monthly look expensive and the yearly commitment look logical.

---

## 4. Price objection handling

### Good response pattern

1. acknowledge
2. understand context
3. compare value
4. clarify outcome
5. offer structure if needed

### Example

> I understand price matters. Before we talk numbers, what outcome matters most to you? If the outcome is clear, I can show you the package that fits the budget and the level of support you want.

---

## 5. Budget-conscious prospect

Do not force the highest package.

Use:

* Bronze
* Silver
* Gold

Then ask:

> Which level makes the most sense for the outcome you want?

# 24) AI Feedback Loop

Then ask AI to tell you:

* what worked
* what failed
* where the buyer leaned in
* where the buyer pulled back
* what objections repeated
* what phrases created trust
* what phrases created resistance

## What AI might say on winning calls

* you asked good diagnosis questions
* you did not rush to the product
* your insight changed how they thought
* the offer was clear
* the next step was specific

## What AI might say on losing calls

* you talked too early
* you did not fully understand the problem
* you jumped to the solution
* you handled objections one by one instead of structurally
* you did not close for a clear next step

## Useful AI prompts

* “Compare winning and losing calls and find the main differences.”
* “What objections appear most often?”
* “Where did I lose control of the conversation?”
* “Which line best created trust?”
* “Where should I have used a stronger insight?”
* “Where did I sound too pushy?”
* “Where did I fail to ask for the next step?”

## Concrete example output

### Winning call pattern

* prospect explained the pain clearly

* you offered a clean method
* you showed a visual demo
* you asked for a decision directly

### Losing call pattern

* you started with the product
* you over-explained features
* you answered objections too fast
* you did not define the next step

# 25) Bad-vs-Good Call Transcript

This is the single most useful practical page.

## Scenario

Clinic owner inquiry. You are selling an automation system.

### Bad call transcript

**Seller:** Hello, thanks for booking. We have a powerful AI system for clinics that can do patient follow-up, reminders, booking automation, analytics, and more.

**Buyer:** Okay. What exactly does it do?

**Seller:** It can handle messages, send reminders, route inquiries, and reduce workload.

**Buyer:** Hmm. We already use WhatsApp. How is this different?

**Seller:** It is much better. We use advanced logic and dashboards.

**Buyer:** Right. But how much does it cost?

**Seller:** Before price, let me tell you all the features.

**Buyer:** I am not sure I need all this. Maybe later.

### Why this fails

* jumped to solution immediately
* no diagnosis
* no basket filling
* no insight
* no method
* no clear outcome
* no trust
* no visual framing
* no close for next step

---

### Good call transcript

**Seller:** Thanks for making time. Before I show anything, let me understand your current setup. How do patient inquiries come in now?

**Buyer:** Mostly WhatsApp and phone calls.

**Seller:** And what usually goes wrong?

**Buyer:** Replies are delayed. Some people never respond. Follow-up is inconsistent.

**Seller:** So the current situation is that leads come in, but some are leaking because response and follow-up are not systemized. What would the ideal outcome be for you?

**Buyer:** More bookings, less manual work, and fewer missed patients.

**Seller:** That makes sense. The main issue is not just traffic. It is leakage after the inquiry. Clinics usually assume the problem is demand, but often the real problem is response speed and follow-up structure.

**Buyer:** That sounds right.

**Seller:** The method is simple: capture inquiry, respond fast, route the lead, follow up automatically, then move qualified patients to booking. Let me show you the workflow visually.

**Buyer:** Okay.

**Seller:** Here is the system in three tiers: Basic, Silver, and Gold. Basic handles core replies. Silver adds automation and reminders. Gold includes full setup and support. Based on what you said, the Silver tier probably fits best.

**Buyer:** What about price?

**Seller:** Price matters. Before I answer, is there anything else besides price?

**Buyer:** I am also not sure about implementation time.

**Seller:** Good. I will answer both together. For implementation, we keep the scope tight. For price, the question is whether the system reduces enough leakage to pay for itself. Let me show you the outcome math.

**Buyer:** Okay, that helps.

**Seller:** What time should we reconnect to finalize this?

### Why this works

* diagnosis first
* clear present situation
* clear prize
* clear problem
* insight before method
* method before solution
* visuals used
* objections collected
* offer anchored
* next step set

# 26) Ghosting After Demo

This is a daily reality.

## Situation

They like the demo.
They say:

* looks good
* let me think
* I will get back to you
  Then they vanish.

## What to do

Do not send one vague follow-up and stop.
Use a simple ghost-busting sequence.

### Ghost-busting script 1

> Just checking whether this is still relevant for you. If it is, I can send the next step. If not, I will close this out.

### Ghost-busting script 2

> You saw the demo. I want to be direct: do you want to continue or should I stop here?

### Ghost-busting script 3

> You mentioned you would get back to me. I am assuming one of three things: timing is bad, it is not a fit, or you want more information. Which one is it?

## Why this works

It removes the fog.
It forces the lead to self-identify.
It saves time.

# 27) Reactivation Cadence and Volume

This was missing and matters a lot.

## Basic rule

Do not reactivation-message forever.
Use a structured sequence.

## Suggested cadence

### Day 0

Silent lead sits in CRM.

### Day 30 of silence

First reactivation message.

### Day 44 of silence

Second message if no response.

### Day 60 of silence

Final reactivation message.

### After that

Move to long-term nurture or archive.

## Channel strategy

Use the channel that best matches the lead’s original behavior.

### If they came from email

* email first
* email second
* email final

### If they came from WhatsApp

* WhatsApp first
* WhatsApp second
* then email if available

### If they came from a call/demo

* message first
* call if appropriate
* then email summary

## Volume rule

A silent lead should get a few real attempts, not endless spam.

### Good pattern

* 3 reactivation attempts over ~30–60 days
* then stop active chasing

### Bad pattern

* 12 messages with no structure
* desperation messaging
* annoying follow-up without reason

# 28) Worst-case Rejection Handling

This was missing too.

## Objection type 1: “This is useless.”

### Bad response

* defending for 10 minutes
* arguing with their emotion

### Better response

> Sounds like this is not matching what you need right now. What would have to be true for it to be useful?

This turns insult into a diagnostic question.

---

## Objection type 2: “I do not trust AI.”

### Better response

> That is fair. The AI is not the point. The point is whether the workflow saves time, reduces leakage, or improves recall. We can look at the process first, not the hype.

### What this does

* removes AI hype pressure
* shifts to outcome
* lowers resistance

---

## Objection type 3: “I had a bad experience with a system like this before.”

### Better response

> That matters. What failed last time: implementation, support, usability, or outcome?

### Why this works

It moves the discussion from emotion to specifics.

---

## Objection type 4: “We tried something similar already.”

### Better response

> Good. What part worked and what part broke?

This avoids looking like you ignored their history.

---

## Objection type 5: “I do not believe this will work for us.”

### Better response

> Fair. What part feels least believable: the workflow, the implementation, or the result?

This lets you isolate the real objection.

---

## Rule for hostile objections

Do not match their tone.
Do not get defensive.
Do not try to win the argument.
Use calm diagnostic questions.

# 29) Post-Call Self-Audit Checklist

Fill this after every presentation.

## 5 questions

* Did I diagnose before offering solution? (Y/N)
* Did I fill all 3 baskets? (Y/N)
* Did I state the insight before the method? (Y/N)
* Did I collect objections before answering? (Y/N)
* Did I set a specific next step with time? (Y/N)

## Scoring rule

* If all 5 are Yes → real attempt
* If any are No → practice rep

## Why this matters

This creates accountability.
It stops fake progress.
It shows where the call broke.

## After a bad call ask

* Where did I rush?
* Where did I skip diagnosis?
* Where did I talk too early?
* Which basket was weak?
* What objection did I mishandle?
* Did I leave with a next step?

# 30) Diagnostic Dashboard Logic

The dashboard should not just track numbers. It should tell you what to fix.

## Basic dashboard

```text
Leads → Appointments → Presentations → Sales
```

## Example diagnostic logic

### Case 1: Leads are high, appointments are low

Example: 50 leads, 3 appointments.

### Diagnosis

* qualification is weak
* booking script is weak
* CTA is unclear
* lead source quality may be bad

### Fix

* tighten lead filters
* improve booking message
* make appointment request specific

---

### Case 2: Appointments are high, presentations are low

Example: 10 appointments, 4 presentations.

### Diagnosis

* no-shows
* poor reminders
* weak commitment
* scheduling friction

### Fix

* send confirmation
* use reminders
* make calendar friction lower
* confirm the value of showing up

---

### Case 3: Presentations are high, sales are low

Example: 6 presentations, 0 or 1 sale.

### Diagnosis

* weak diagnosis
* weak insight
* weak offer
* pricing or packaging issue
* objections handled poorly

### Fix

* improve call structure
* use better insight
* improve offer tiers
* use objection collection method

---

### Case 4: Sales are low despite good calls

### Diagnosis

* follow-up is weak
* ghosting not handled
* no reactivation
* bad timing

### Fix

* tighten follow-up sequence
* add ghost-busting scripts
* add reactivation cadence
* set next step before ending calls
